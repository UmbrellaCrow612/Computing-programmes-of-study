
_The Big Book of Computing Pedagogy_ is a special edition of _Hello World_ magazine, produced by the Raspberry Pi Foundation, that serves as a comprehensive guide for teaching computing in the classroom. It is designed to bridge the gap between educational research and practical classroom application by presenting "bite-sized" evidence-based resources.

### **Structure and Purpose**

The book is structured around **twelve pedagogical principles** originally developed for the National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE) in England. Its primary purpose is to act as a companion for educators, offering tried-and-tested approaches that are applicable to both novice and experienced teachers globally. The content typically includes:

- **Research Summaries**: Accessible explanations of current computing education research.
- **Practitioner Stories**: Real-world accounts from educators who have implemented these research-based strategies in their own teaching spaces.

### **The Twelve Pedagogical Principles**

While the document details various frameworks, the principles are designed to focus on the best ways to approach the teaching and learning of computing. Key principles and models highlighted include:

- **Lead with Concepts**: Focus on teaching the underlying rich concepts (like variables or binary) rather than getting distracted by specific tools or languages.
- **Structure Lessons**: Use established frameworks to guide the learning journey. Examples include:
    - **PRIMM**: Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make—a model specifically for programmin
    - **Use-Modify-Create**: A framework that helps learners progress from using expert examples to creating their own projects.
- **Unplugged Activities**: Introducing computing concepts without a computer (e.g., using physical objects or storytelling) to help break down abstract ideas.
- **Physical Computing**: Using hardware like the Raspberry Pi to interact with the real world, which provides a tangible way to understand program comprehension.
- **Collaboration and Communication**: Encouraging techniques like **pair programming** and "exploratory talk" to develop social skills and peer-to-peer troubleshooting.
- **Foster Accessibility**: Applying **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)** to make computing inclusive by providing multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression.
- **Read Before Write**: Emphasizing that students should be able to read and trace code before they are expected to write it independently.
- **Semantic Waves**: A strategy where teachers move between abstract technical terms and simple, concrete explanations to help students build understanding.
- **Variety in Assessment**: Using diverse strategies (such as code reviews, portfolios, or multiple-choice questions) depending on whether the teaching lens is engineering, math, or science-focused.
